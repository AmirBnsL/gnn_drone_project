# Dataset Sampling Guidance for PID Imitation with PyG

This note summarizes the reasoning behind dataset sizing and temporal sampling for the GNN dataset generated by `data_collection.py`.

## What an Episode and a Step Mean

- An **episode** is one full simulation rollout with a newly sampled number of drones, initial positions, initial yaws, and one chosen task or formation target.
- A **step** is one call to `env.step()`, which in this codebase is one control-loop update.
- In the current generator, each collected step becomes one PyTorch Geometric `Data` graph.

So if `num_episodes=200` and `max_steps=300`, the total number of saved graph samples is about:

$$
200 \times 300 = 60{,}000
$$

## Timing Interpretation

- `Aviary` physics runs at `240 Hz`.
- `Aviary.step()` advances one control-loop step.
- The generator documentation states samples are collected at the controller loop rate, effectively `120 Hz`.

That means one saved graph is about:

$$
\frac{1}{120} \approx 8.3 \text{ ms}
$$

apart from the next one.

This is why neighboring samples can become highly correlated, especially late in an episode when the drones are already close to the target formation.

## What the Current Dataset Actually Captures

For formation tasks, each episode does this:

1. Randomly samples drone count and initial states.
2. Chooses one formation target for the whole episode.
3. Records one graph per step while the PID controller drives the drones toward that target.

This means the steps inside one episode usually move through three phases:

1. Early transient: drones are scattered and making large corrections.
2. Mid convergence: drones are organizing into formation.
3. Late stabilization: drones are near the target and behavior is highly repetitive.

## Main Dataset Design Principle

For this task, **more episodes are usually more valuable than more steps**.

Reason:

- More episodes increase scenario diversity.
- More steps mainly increase temporal density inside the same scenario.
- Since neighboring steps are close in time, long episodes can overrepresent near-duplicate steady-state behavior.

For the same total graph budget, this is usually better:

- `200 episodes x 240 steps = 48,000 graphs`

than this:

- `50 episodes x 960 steps = 48,000 graphs`

because the first option covers many more initial conditions and drone-team layouts.

## Recommended Ranges by Use Case

### 1. Fast Formation Acquisition

Use when the goal is to learn recovery from random initial positions and fast convergence.

- `num_episodes=200` to `500`
- `max_steps=120` to `300`

Why:

- More independent starts.
- Less dataset budget wasted on long settled tails.

### 2. Balanced Acquisition and Stabilization

Use when the goal is both reaching formation and holding it briefly.

- `num_episodes=100` to `300`
- `max_steps=240` to `480`

Why:

- Captures transient behavior plus early near-equilibrium control.
- Good default starting point for imitation learning.

### 3. Stable Formation Maintenance

Use when the goal is mainly steady holding once formation is achieved.

- `num_episodes=50` to `150`
- `max_steps=600` to `1200`

Why:

- Late steady-state behavior becomes the focus.

Risk:

- Can weaken recovery behavior if used alone.

### 4. Wind or Sensor-Noise Robustness

Use when training under disturbances.

- `num_episodes=200+`
- `max_steps=300` to `600`

Why:

- Disturbances add useful variability within episodes.
- Longer episodes are more justified than in the disturbance-free case.

### 5. Mixed-Formation Generalization

Use when one GNN should handle `a`, `rectangle`, and `triangle`.

- `num_episodes=300+`
- `max_steps=240` to `400`

Why:

- Need enough episodes per formation type.
- Need enough random initial conditions per type.

## Practical Default Recommendation

For the current project, a strong initial baseline is:

- `dataset_type="mixed_formations"`
- `num_episodes=200`
- `max_steps=240` to `300`
- `communication_radius=10.0`
- `include_formation_in_state=True`

Why this is a good default:

- About `2` to `2.5` seconds of control horizon at `120 Hz`.
- Enough time for meaningful convergence.
- Not so long that steady-state dominates the dataset.
- Enough scenario diversity to train a more general model.

## Is Non-Uniform Temporal Sampling Valid?

Yes. It is methodologically sound to keep more steps early in the episode and fewer after convergence.

Reason:

- Early samples carry more information because state and control are changing quickly.
- Late samples are often highly correlated and therefore less useful per stored graph.

This does **not** make the dataset invalid. It is often a better use of storage and training budget.

The only tradeoff is behavioral emphasis:

- Dense early sampling improves recovery and convergence learning.
- Dense late sampling improves stable formation maintenance.

So the right design is usually a **tapered schedule**, not dropping the late phase entirely.

## Recommended Tapered Sampling Policy

At `120 Hz`, a good first policy is:

1. Keep every step for the first `120` steps.
2. Keep every `2nd` step for the next `120` steps.
3. Keep every `4th` or `5th` step after that.

Interpretation in time:

- first `120` steps: first `1` second
- next `120` steps: second `1` second
- after `240` steps: later stabilization region

This preserves the transient phase at full resolution while reducing redundant near-steady-state samples.

## When to Prefer More Early Sampling

- Random initial drone layouts.
- Formation acquisition is the main goal.
- Storage or training time is limited.
- Mixed formations are used and scenario diversity matters more than long steady tails.

## When to Keep More Late Sampling

- Fine stabilization matters.
- Low-error hovering behavior matters.
- Wind or disturbances make the late-phase dynamics still informative.
- The model already converges well but jitters near the target.

## What Not to Use as the Final Dataset by Default

These are okay for quick tests, but not ideal as the main training set:

- `20 episodes x 400 steps`
  - Too few independent rollouts for strong generalization.
- Very long episodes with no disturbances
  - Tend to overfill the dataset with steady-state duplicates.

## A Simple Rule of Thumb

If the dataset budget is fixed:

1. Increase `num_episodes` first.
2. Increase `max_steps` second.
3. Use temporal downsampling when late-stage samples become redundant.

## Recommended Starting Points

### Minimum Viable Baseline

- `num_episodes=100`
- `max_steps=240`
- uniform step sampling

### Strong Baseline

- `num_episodes=200`
- `max_steps=300`
- mixed formations
- optional tapered step sampling

### Robustness-Oriented Variant

- `num_episodes=250` to `400`
- `max_steps=300` to `600`
- enable wind and/or sensor noise

## Practical Dataset Design Additions

These are high-value implementation details for this project.

### Store Rollout Metadata Per Graph

Each saved PyG graph should include at least:

- `episode_id`
- `step_idx`
- `num_drones`

Why:

- Makes it possible to split, filter, and analyze the dataset safely.
- Lets you evaluate early-step vs late-step behavior.
- Lets you track performance by team size.

### Split by Episode, Never by Step

Train, validation, and test splits should be created at the episode level.

Do **not** mix steps from the same rollout across different splits.

Why:

- Adjacent steps are highly correlated.
- Graph-level random splitting would leak rollout information into validation and test.
- Episode-level splitting gives a more honest estimate of generalization.

### Use Mixed Formations as the Default Generalization Setting

If the goal is one model that works across multiple formations, the default dataset should use:

- `dataset_type="mixed_formations"`
- more episodes,
- moderate rollout length,
- tapered temporal sampling.

Why:

- Mixed formations increase task diversity.
- More episodes improve initial-condition coverage.
- Tapered sampling reduces redundant late-rollout storage.

### Build Harder Validation Subsets on Purpose

Validation should not only use unseen seeds. It should also be slightly harder than training.

Practical way to do this:

- use a separate seed stream for validation and test episodes,
- increase the initial XY spread for validation,
- increase it further for test if desired.

Why:

- This tests generalization to harder initial conditions, not just replay quality on familiar rollout statistics.

### Save a Sidecar Metadata File

Each dataset generation run should write a small metadata file next to the `.pt` files.

It should record:

- split file names,
- generator parameters,
- split ratios,
- seed policy,
- tapered-sampling configuration,
- per-episode summary records.

Why:

- Makes the dataset reproducible.
- Prevents ambiguity about how a given training run was produced.

## Suggested Generator Defaults

For this repository, a practical default recipe is:

- `dataset_type="mixed_formations"`
- `num_episodes=200` to `240`
- `max_steps=240` to `300`
- `split_ratios=(0.8, 0.1, 0.1)`
- `tapered_sampling=True`
- `dense_sampling_steps=120`
- `mid_sampling_steps=240`
- `mid_step_stride=2`
- `late_step_stride=5`
- `validation_spread_scale=1.25`
- `test_spread_scale=1.5`

This gives:

- episode-level train/val/test splits,
- unseen-seed validation and test rollouts,
- harder validation subsets,
- lower redundancy in late rollout stages,
- a reproducible sidecar metadata record.

## Final Takeaway

For a GNN imitating a PID formation controller, the dataset should usually emphasize:

1. many rollout scenarios,
2. enough horizon to capture convergence,
3. less aggressive storage of highly correlated late steady-state samples.

That is why a tapered step-sampling policy is both valid and often preferable.
